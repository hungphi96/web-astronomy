Dirty Snow Ball

Interesting facts about the stars !

Every star that the human eye can see in the sky is many times larger and brighter than the Sun. Of the 50 brightest stars visible to the naked eye on Earth, the weakest is Alpha Centauri. However, it is still 1.5 times brighter than the Sun and cannot be easily seen in the northern hemisphere.

On nights without the moon or any other light source around, a person with good eyesight can see about 2,000 - 2,500 stars at a time. So, if someone says they see millions of stars in the sky, that's just an exaggeration.

STAR VIEW LOCATIONS

1.Brecon Beacons, Wales : During the day, the area is barren with high humidity, but when the sky is clear, the Brecon Beacons site is well worth the long way here. Wales' first 'night sky reserve' has effectively reduced light pollution with the participation of local communities, enabling meteor showers, nebulae and, rarely, meteor showers. Northern Lights in excellent condition. You can bring binoculars to Hay Bluff or to the atmospheric ruins of medieval Llanthony Abbey to experience starry nights.

2.Pic du Midi Observatory, Haute-Pyrénées, France: Hiding in the clouds amid the spectacular peaks of the Pyrenees, Pic du Midi is an extraordinary spot to watch the sky day and night. As the sunset
gradually erased the entire mountain landscape, the bright stars covered the sky like a shimmering carpet.

3.Atacama Desert, Chile: The dry conditions and low cloud cover, combined with an altitude of 5000m, make it one of the world's top places for space observation. International scientists use high-tech satellites at the ALMA Observatory - home to the world's largest astronomical project - to "search for the origins of our universe" with antennas built clustered in clusters in the Atacama Desert facing the sky.

4.Mauna Kea, Hawaii: Located more than 4,200m above sea level and with a year-round dry climate, the summit of Mauna Kea – a dormant volcano on the southernmost island of Hawaii – is ideal for astronomical observations. 13 giant observatories and telescopes owned by 11 different countries appear on the top of Mauna Kea with the aim of making it possible for scientists to perceive light from distant galaxies.
